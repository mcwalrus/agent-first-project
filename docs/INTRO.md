# Introduction

Geoffrey Huntley is known as the original founder the technique for putting agents into a loop.

To quote Huntley:

> “In the previous 12 months, I've cloned SaaS product feature sets of many different companies. I've built file systems, networking protocols and even developed my own programming language. From my perspective, nothing really changed in December. The models were already great, but what was needed was a time of rest - people just needed to pick up the guitar and play.”

Take Simon Willison, a leading SFO software engineer and leading AI research communicator:

> “Becoming a professional software engineer is hard. Getting good enough for people to pay you money to write software takes years of dedicated work. The rewards are significant: this is a well compensated career which opens up a lot of great opportunities … The idea that this could all be stripped away by a chatbot is deeply upsetting.”

I intend to present how these realities and outcomes are achievable for yourself.

While I, like many software engineers, am directly affected by these developments, the implications stretch far beyond our industry. AI is disrupting all knowledge-based work simultaneously — and anyone willing to adapt stands to benefit, at least for now. But that window feels uncertain when our economy depends on human labour across every sector. If agents displace that labour at scale, the question of what's left for the rest of us becomes unavoidable. For software engineers, the role has quietly shifted into something strange: using AI to automate not just our own work, but everyone else's too.

My goal in presenting this is to make people aware. I believe what is happening deserves serious political conversation. Almost no-one has noticed the cost of AI-driven software development already operates below minimum wage in cost. No human worker no matter how skilled or how willing to adapt can compete with that on price. Previous technological disruptions displaced certain kinds of work, but they also created new roles, new industries, new demand for human effort. This time things are fundamentally different. With AI, we have a technology capable of making autonomous decisions itself. One that can act as a personal assistant, a business manager, or effectively an entire company operating end-to-end if achitected well.

I hold this as an existential concern. Nations that realise this understand than those who control governance and access to the models will hold the most power. Without credible intervention, we may quietly see the erosion and disappearance of our workforce. One that pays back to society through it's income tax. How would ecconomies look to support themselves when this cat is out of the bag. I believe there is a fundamental change in what the social contract of money is, that is, the idea that "money represents work done". If I work for you, then I pay you, and I hope in return I can run or maintain my business. What happens when we all start going to the model providers out of a shared awareness in our pursuit of growth?

I will thank you for time in engaging in this approach to software development.
